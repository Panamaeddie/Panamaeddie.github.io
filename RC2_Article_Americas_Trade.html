Executive Summary

The Americas represent the most strategically important supply chain theater for U.S. apparel, textile, and manufacturing industries in 2026 ‚Äî and the most legally complex. Following the Supreme Court's February 2026 invalidation of IEEPA tariffs, the tariff stack across the Western Hemisphere has been reshuffled: USMCA remains the gold standard of duty-free access; CAFTA-DR countries now face a 10% overlay despite FTA compliance; Haiti's HOPE/HELP programs were retroactively restored through December 2026; and South American FTA partners face a narrowed but real tariff burden. Brazil faces unique political and economic exposure. The AAPN region ‚Äî spanning the U.S., Mexico, Caribbean, Central America, and South America ‚Äî is simultaneously the most affected and best-positioned region to capture competitive advantage as global sourcing realigns away from Asia. This briefing maps the current tariff landscape country by country, analyzes each active trade agreement, and delivers a clear operational playbook for businesses navigating this terrain.

AAPN

What Is AAPN and Why Does It Define the 2026 Sourcing Conversation?

The Americas Apparel Producers' Network (AAPN), organized in 1981 and headquartered in Mount Pleasant, SC, is the premier private-sector, non-profit business network for the Western Hemisphere textile and apparel supply chain. With 600+ company owners and senior executives from nearly 200 organizations, AAPN connects all 30 links of the supply chain ‚Äî from fiber and yarn to retail ‚Äî across the U.S., Mexico, Caribbean, Central America, and South America.

AAPN's geographic scope precisely maps to the most strategically advantaged sourcing region in the world right now. As Asian import tariffs remain elevated (10‚Äì50%+ depending on country), the AAPN region ‚Äî protected by USMCA, CAFTA-DR, the Caribbean Basin preference programs, and bilateral FTAs ‚Äî offers a unique combination of geographic proximity, trade agreement protection, and compliance infrastructure that no other sourcing theater can match in 2026.

The Critical Issue CAFTA-DR Clients Must Understand

The single most important nuance for AAPN-region clients that many practitioners are missing: CAFTA-DR compliance does not exempt goods from the Section 122 10% tariff. This is a significant operational and cost reality with no modern precedent.

A cotton t-shirt (HTS 6109.10.00) with a standard MFN rate of 16.5% ‚Äî previously entering duty-free under CAFTA-DR compliance ‚Äî now enters at approximately 26.5% effective rate: 16.5% MFN base + 10% Section 122 overlay. This applies to Honduras, Nicaragua, Costa Rica, and the Dominican Republic today. Only Guatemala and El Salvador have announced (but not yet implemented) duty-free framework agreements. (Source: Worker Rights Consortium, July 2025; Reed Smith, February 2026)

This creates a pricing and competitiveness gap that demands immediate recalculation. Brands and retailers sourcing from the CAFTA-DR region must rebuild every landed cost model, renegotiate supplier contracts to address the new tariff pass-through reality, and reassess the CAFTA-DR region's competitive position against Mexico (0%) and other sourcing options. The National Council of Textile Organizations is actively lobbying for full restoration of CAFTA-DR duty-free status. The path forward ‚Äî either a Section 122 exemption for CAFTA-DR or individual framework agreements for each country ‚Äî is the most consequential near-term trade policy question for the AAPN region.

Panama Canal: The Supply Chain Wildcard

No Americas trade briefing is complete without the Panama Canal. Approximately 5% of all world trade passes through the canal. Trump administration pressure on Panama's canal management ‚Äî citing Chinese investment in ports on both ends and sovereignty concerns ‚Äî has introduced a geopolitical overlay on what is fundamentally a logistics infrastructure question.

The Panama Canal is not a tariff issue ‚Äî it is a logistics infrastructure and geopolitical security issue. For any AAPN-region business that sources from Asia and routes through Panama, monitoring canal access, transit fees, and U.S.‚ÄìPanama political relations is as critical as monitoring tariff policy itself.

The AAPN Region's Strategic Moment

Despite its disruptions, the tariff environment of 2025‚Äì2026 represents the most significant strategic opportunity for Western Hemisphere manufacturing in a generation. Asian manufacturing faces tariff headwinds of 10‚Äì50%+ depending on country and product. USMCA-compliant Mexican production faces zero. CAFTA-DR + HOPE/HELP production faces 0‚Äì26% depending on framework agreement status. The proximity advantage ‚Äî faster lead times, lower logistics costs, smaller minimum orders, stronger social compliance visibility ‚Äî now has a tariff advantage to match it.

"Latin America supplies commodities and mid-tier manufactures that the U.S. needs. Proximity will always count. The U.S. at full employment cannot credibly substitute for these imports. The AAPN region's structural advantages are not temporary ‚Äî they are being amplified by trade policy that cannot realistically be sustained at Asian tariff levels." ‚Äî Americas Quarterly, February 2026

The Americas Operational Playbook

🇲🇽 USMCA Optimization

  • Audit Mexico-origin goods for USMCA rule of origin compliance ‚Äî non-qualifying goods lose duty-free status entirely
  • Document yarn-forward or fiber-forward origin for all textiles and apparel
  • Monitor USMCA 2026 review ‚Äî prepare for potential rules of origin revision
  • Evaluate nearshoring investment in Mexico for high-tariff import categories
  • Maintain labor compliance documentation ‚Äî USMCA rapid response mechanism is active

🌿 CAFTA-DR Strategy

  • Recalculate every landed cost for CAFTA-DR-origin goods under 10% Sec. 122 overlay
  • Prioritize Guatemala and El Salvador sourcing expansion as framework agreements finalize
  • Maintain full CAFTA-DR origin documentation ‚Äî framework agreements require verified qualifying origin
  • Monitor Honduras, D.R., and Costa Rica framework agreement announcements weekly
  • Review Mexico Cumulation provisions under Chapter 98 ‚Äî underutilized tool for apparel

🇭🇹 Haiti HOPE/HELP

  • File retroactive refund (PSC or protest) by August 2, 2026 for duties paid Oct. 1, 2025 ‚Äì Feb. 3, 2026
  • Use SPI "D" designation for all qualifying Haiti entries from Feb. 9, 2026 forward
  • Track weekly TPL quota fill rates at CBP.gov ‚Äî quotas can fill mid-year
  • Engage AAFA and NCTO on long-term HOPE/HELP extension advocacy
  • Build contingency sourcing plan for December 2026 program expiration

🌎 South America FTA Partners

  • Update landed cost models for Colombia, Peru, Chile, and Panama to reflect 10% Section 122 addition
  • For Peru and Chile copper sourcing ‚Äî calculate Section 232 (25%+) separately from Section 122
  • Track Section 122 expiration (~July 2026) ‚Äî the key policy inflection for FTA partners
  • Deepen supplier relationships in Colombia to hedge against China pivot risk
  • Assess Ecuador and Argentina framework agreement products for duty savings

🇧🇷 Brazil Risk Management

  • Avoid single-source dependency on Brazilian manufacturing inputs
  • If IEEPA tariffs were paid on Brazilian goods in 2025 ‚Äî assess refund eligibility immediately
  • Monitor Bolsonaro case resolution ‚Äî U.S.‚ÄìBrazil political relations are volatile
  • Agricultural imports from Brazil (beef, citrus) ‚Äî check Section 122 exemption status
  • Steel imports from Brazil ‚Äî calculate Section 232 (50%) exposure separately

📋 Compliance Infrastructure

  • Build a Western Hemisphere trade agreement matrix ‚Äî every supplier mapped to agreement status and current effective rate
  • Ensure customs broker uses post-IEEPA HTS schedule ‚Äî many brokers have not updated
  • Create 90-day monitoring calendar: CAFTA-DR frameworks, Section 122 expiration, USMCA review, Haiti HOPE/HELP extension status
  • Train procurement teams on rules of origin ‚Äî the most commonly mismanaged compliance area across all Americas sourcing programs
  • Document all tariff payment periods ‚Äî IEEPA refund windows are time-limited and non-extendable
