Nearshoring to the Americas: Operational Risks & Readiness Factors

RC2 Consulting | February 2026 | 10 min read
← Back to Resources

Executive Summary

Shifting trade dynamics, rising tariffs, and supply chain instability are accelerating a nearshoring movement toward Latin America. However, the operational and compliance risks of this transition are consistently underestimated. Facilities entering U.S. supply chains face a compressed readiness timeline: WRAP certification, customs compliance, workforce documentation, and buyer-imposed codes of conduct must be addressed simultaneously. This article provides a Triple-Horizon analysis of the nearshoring landscape — what companies face today, what infrastructure they must build over the next 12–18 months, and what long-term compliance posture a successful nearshoring strategy requires.

The Nearshoring Acceleration

The reshaping of global supply chains is no longer a forecast — it is a documented, accelerating reality. Escalating U.S.-China trade tensions, Section 301 tariffs, and post-pandemic supply chain fragility have driven a fundamental shift in sourcing strategy among U.S. importers. Latin America — particularly Honduras, Guatemala, El Salvador, Mexico, and Colombia — has emerged as the primary nearshoring destination for apparel, manufacturing, and light industrial production.

According to the U.S. International Trade Commission, nearshoring activity to CAFTA-DR countries increased by 23% between 2022 and 2025, with apparel and textile production leading the shift. The World Bank projects continued acceleration through 2028, driven by labor cost advantages, proximity-based logistics savings, and preferential trade agreement access. (Source: USITC, 2025; World Bank Trade Report, 2024)

The decision to nearshore is a strategic one. The failure to prepare operationally and compliance-wise before entering the U.S. supply chain is an operational one — and it is avoidable.

The Readiness Gap — What Companies Get Wrong

The most consistent failure pattern in nearshoring transitions is not production capacity — it is compliance and operational readiness. Companies that successfully build or acquire production infrastructure in Latin America frequently underestimate the compliance infrastructure required to access U.S. buyers, government contracts, and major retail supply chains.

The readiness gap manifests in five specific areas:

1. Social Compliance Certification

WRAP certification is the most widely recognized social compliance standard in the apparel and sewn products industry. U.S. buyers — including major retailers, brands, and government contractors — require WRAP certification or equivalent as a baseline condition of supply chain participation. Facilities entering the U.S. supply chain without this certification are effectively locked out of the most valuable buyer relationships.

2. Customs & Trade Compliance

CAFTA-DR preferential tariff treatment requires rigorous country-of-origin documentation, regional value content verification, and customs compliance record-keeping. Companies that fail to establish these systems before their first shipment face retroactive tariff exposure, customs holds, and potential debarment from preferential treatment programs.

3. Workforce Documentation Standards

U.S. buyers impose workforce documentation requirements that frequently exceed local legal minimums. Wage records, hour logs, age verification documentation, worker grievance procedures, and anti-harassment policies must all be maintained in retrievable, auditable formats — in both local language and English.

4. Environmental & Safety Compliance

Environmental compliance requirements — wastewater management, chemical handling, fire safety — are audit points in virtually every major social compliance standard. Facilities built to local code frequently fall short of international buyer standards and require investment before certification is achievable.

5. Management Systems & SOP Infrastructure

Auditors do not just review conditions — they review systems. A facility without documented management systems, written SOPs, internal audit protocols, and corrective action tracking will fail a WRAP or equivalent audit regardless of actual conditions on the floor.

Triple-Horizon Analysis — The Nearshoring Compliance Timeline

Horizon 1 — Pre-Entry (0–6 Mo.)
Gap Assessment & Foundation

Conduct compliance gap assessment. Identify documentation deficiencies. Map buyer requirements. Begin SOP development and policy documentation. Engage WRAP-registered monitor.

Horizon 2 — Ramp (6–18 Mo.)
Build & Certify

Complete SOP library. Train workforce and management. Conduct pre-audit internal review. Submit WRAP application. Establish customs compliance documentation system.

Horizon 3 — Scale (18–36 Mo.)
Sustain & Expand

Maintain certification through annual audits. Expand buyer relationships. Build multi-facility compliance systems. Develop internal compliance team capability.

Conclusion

Nearshoring to Latin America represents a genuine strategic opportunity — one that is being realized by hundreds of U.S. companies navigating a fundamentally changed trade environment. But the opportunity is only accessible to companies that arrive in the U.S. supply chain with their compliance and operational infrastructure already built.

The companies that succeed in nearshoring transitions are not the ones that move fastest. They are the ones that move most prepared.
